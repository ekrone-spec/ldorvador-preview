The full story · 375 years

A tropical island at the heart of Jewish history

← Back home

The island's Jewish presence rarely appears in mainstream tourism narratives, yet it is embedded in the very design of UNESCO-listed Willemstad — in street names, family lineages, merchant houses built by Sephardic traders, and wrought-iron gates marked with Stars of David. Even Papiamentu, the local language, carries the Portuguese brought to the island by the Sephardim.

They were farmers on paper, but merchants at heart — the island was never really about the soil; it was about the harbour.

The story begins with Samuel Cohen, who arrived in 1634 with the Dutch West India Company fleet that took Curaçao from Spain — generally regarded as the first Jew to set foot on the island. In 1651, a small handful of families were authorised to found Plantation De Hoop — "The Hope" — and Congregation Mikvé Israel. They came with the instincts of a port people: prayer books in hand, trade languages on their tongues. When the arid soil proved hard to farm, they turned to the skill their Atlantic networks made possible, transforming Curaçao into a commercial hub linking Europe, the Caribbean, and South America.

When the Portuguese retook the Brazilian ports, Jews were expelled almost as quickly as they had been invited. Families fled again — packing trunks, rolling Torah scrolls — scattering to Amsterdam and Caribbean havens, with a small, famous group pushing north to New Amsterdam, modern-day lower Manhattan. Those 23 Sephardic refugees founded the Mill Street Synagogue — later Congregation Shearith Israel, the oldest congregation in North America, only three years younger than Curaçao's.

In 2026, Curaçao celebrates 375 years of continuous Jewish life.

Beside the historic synagogue, the newly expanded Jewish Museum Curaçao blends centuries-old manuscripts and ritual objects — carried across oceans and through two World Wars — with interactive storytelling. Later waves of Ashkenazi families added their own melodies and rituals, founding congregations such as Shaarei Tsedek. Today, with about two hundred Jews on the island, the community moves fluidly between congregations, sharing in each other's celebrations and milestones.

This is Curaçao's quiet power.

In a region where many Jewish communities now exist mostly in memory, Curaçao remains a place of practice — a museum still adding rooms, a Jewish calendar still kept, an active and growing Chabad House. For centuries, Jews crossed oceans to build this community. To visit is to become part of its continuum.
