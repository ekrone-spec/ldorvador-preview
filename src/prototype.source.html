The full story · hidden in plain sight

A tropical island at the heart of Jewish history

The island's Jewish presence rarely appears in mainstream tourism narratives, yet it is embedded in the very design of UNESCO-listed Willemstad — in street names, family lineages, merchant houses built by Sephardic traders, and wrought-iron gates marked with Stars of David. Even Papiamentu, the local language, carries the Portuguese brought to the island by the Sephardim.

They were farmers on paper, but merchants at heart — the island was never really about the soil; it was about the harbour.

The story begins with Samuel Cohen, who arrived in 1634 with the Dutch West India Company fleet that took Curaçao from Spain — generally regarded as the first Jew to set foot on the island. In 1651, a small handful of families were authorised to found Plantation De Hoop — "The Hope" — and Congregation Mikvé Israel. They came with the instincts of a port people: prayer books in hand, trade languages on their tongues. When the arid soil proved hard to farm, they turned to the skill their Atlantic networks made possible, transforming Curaçao into a commercial hub linking Europe, the Caribbean, and South America.

When the Portuguese retook the Brazilian ports, Jews were expelled almost as quickly as they had been invited. Families fled again — packing trunks, rolling Torah scrolls — scattering to Amsterdam and Caribbean havens, with a small, famous group pushing north to New Amsterdam, modern-day lower Manhattan.

Those 23 Sephardic refugees founded the Mill Street Synagogue — later Congregation Shearith Israel, the oldest congregation in North America, only three years younger than Curaçao's. Long before hundreds of synagogues dotted the continent, there were these two beginnings.

In 2026, Curaçao celebrates 375 years of continuous Jewish life.

Beside the historic synagogue, the newly expanded Jewish Museum Curaçao blends centuries-old manuscripts and ritual objects — carried across oceans and through two World Wars — with interactive storytelling. Later waves of Ashkenazi families added their own melodies and rituals, founding congregations such as Shaarei Tsedek. Today, with about two hundred Jews on the island, the community moves fluidly between congregations, sharing in each other's celebrations and milestones.

This is Curaçao's quiet power.

In a region where many Jewish communities now exist mostly in memory, Curaçao remains a place of practice — a museum still adding rooms, a Jewish calendar still kept, an active and growing Chabad House. For centuries, Jews crossed oceans to build this community. To visit is to become part of its continuum.

A note on our name

"From generation to generation."

L'Dor Vador speaks to the heart of Jewish continuity — the lived, emotional bond that links grandparents to grandchildren, ancestors to descendants, through memory, tradition, and shared identity. It appears throughout Jewish liturgy and carries the sacred responsibility of passing down history and belonging. For us, it captures what we offer: journeys that reconnect travellers with the roots of Jewish life in Curaçao, and a chance to find one's own place within the transatlantic Jewish story.

Our story

Founded by a journalist who tells a place by its people.

Hannah Berkeley Cohen is an Ohio native who has called the Caribbean home since 2012. In Havana she became the New York Times' primary stringer, reporting on U.S.–Cuba policy, the death of Fidel Castro, and the historic visits of President Obama and the Pope — and meeting Jewish groups returning to Cuba for the first time in generations.

She founded Cuba Rising, a bespoke travel company revealing the complex realities of modern Cuba, with trips capped at eight for depth and flexibility. In 2024 she moved to Curaçao to delve into the island's Jewish heritage and build culturally-relevant programmes for North American travellers. She is fluent in English and Spanish, and is learning Dutch and Papiamentu.

What to expect: immersive journeys of people-to-people encounters — meeting prominent members of the local Jewish community, storytelling at historic sites, and dialogues with scholars, artists, and cultural trailblazers.

Frequently asked questions

Is Curaçao easy to reach?
Yes. Curaçao has direct flights from Miami, New York, and Amsterdam. We meet you at the airport and handle every transfer from the moment you land.
Do you cater to kosher and Shabbat observance?
Absolutely. We arrange kosher catering in both Sephardic and Ashkenazi traditions, plan around Shabbat and the festivals, and place you within walking distance of the synagogue when you wish.
Is this suitable for older travellers?
It is our specialty. Itineraries are paced gently, distances are short, transport is private and comfortable, and we adapt each day to your energy. Mobility needs are planned in advance.
Can you plan for a synagogue or private group?
Yes — group planning is at the heart of what we do. We handle room blocks, motorcoaches, group meals, and services for congregations and families of any size.
How do payments work?
You reserve your place through us and we send a secure invoice from our trusted booking partner. No card details are ever entered on this website.
